Heat Capacity¬∂

Heat capacity (C) is the energy (E) required to raise the temperature of a body of a material by 1ÀöC, independent of mass.

\(C=\frac{E}{‚àÜT}\) \(JÀöC^{-1}\)

where \(‚àÜT\) is the change in temperature.

Specific heat capacity (c) is the energy needed to heat up 1kg of a material by 1ÀöC.

\(c=\frac{E}{m‚àÜT}\) \(Jkg^{-1}ÀöC^{-1}\)

where m is the mass of the body of a material.

Heat capacity of water >> ice > air > land.

An oversimplified model¬∂

The energy flux density at the surface of the Earth can be calculated from the inverse square law, given the power of the Sun \(3.86\)x\(10^{26}W\) (\(E_S\)), and the distance between the Sun and the Earth \(1.5\)x\(10^{11}m\) (\(R\)):

\[S_o=\frac{E_S}{4\pi R^{2}}\]

This is the solar constant \(S_o\), which is roughly 1365 \(Wm^{-2}\).

Energy balance model is crucial in building a climate model, since atmospheric dynamics are driven by the input of energy into the climate system.

Let‚Äôs first make several assumptions about the Earth:

  1. It is a perfect blackbody

  2. Its albedo is 0.3, which is the average terrestrial albedo

  3. It is in radiative equilibrium i.e. energy in = energy out

  4. Equal solar irradiation on the surface of the Earth

Recall that albedo is the reflectance of an object over all wavelengths, which measures how well a surface reflects EM radiation. Its value is between 0 and 1, for example 0.1 means 10% of EM radiation incident on the object is reflected, so 90% of it is absorbed. Therefore, albedo is higher for ice and snow (0.9) than that for water (0.06).

From here, we can start by calculating the energy flux density on the surface of the Earth.

Earth radius = \(R_E\); albedo = \(Œ±\)

We already know the energy flux density at the surface of the Earth = the solar constant \(S_o\), so the energy flux density including albedo becomes \(S_oA(1-Œ±)\).

Thus, the energy flux density into the Earth is:

\[M_{in}=S_o(1-Œ±)\]

In terms of total energy flux:

\[E_{in}=S_o\pi R_E^2(1-Œ±)\]

The energy flux density emitted is described by Stefan-Boltzmann Law:

\[M_{out}=œÉùëá_K^{4}\]

where œÉ=Stefan-Boltzmann constant=\(5.6697\)x\(10^{-8}Wm^{-2}K^{-4}\), and \(T_K\) is absolute temperature. In terms of total energy flux:

\[E_{out}=4\pi R_E^2œÉùëá_K^{4}\]

At radiative equilibrium, energy absorbed = energy emitted, thus:

\[E_{in}=E_{out}\]
\[S_o\pi R_E^2(1-Œ±)=4\pi R_E^{2}œÉùëá_K^{4}\]

Eliminating \(\pi\) and \(R_E\) gives:

\[\frac{S_o(1-Œ±)}{4}=œÉùëá_K^{4}\]

Rearranging this equation gives the temperature at the surface of the Earth:

\[T_K=(\frac{S_o(1-Œ±)}{4œÉ})^{\frac{1}{4}}\]

Substituting albedo=0.3, temperature on the surface of the Earth is -18ÀöC.

Actually, the assumptions are not valid and are too simple.

Assumption 1¬∂

The Earth is not a perfect blackbody, because it doesn‚Äôt radiate perfectly.

Assumption 2¬∂

70% of the Earth is covered by ocean, and albedo of the ocean is much lower than terrestrial albedo. On the other hand, a around 10% of land is covered by ice. Overall, albedo should be lower than 0.3.

Assumption 3¬∂

Equilibrium is disturbed by greenhouse effect, positive feedbacks, and other factors.

Assumption 4¬∂

The Earth is a sphere, so heat absorbtion (and heat emission) is not uniform.

We‚Äôll now address some of the issues mentioned above.

A less oversimplified model¬∂

To improve the model and address assumption 1, emissivity Œµ is introduced to describe the difference in the range of radiation emitted between the object and an ideal black body.

Including this ‚Äòfudge factor‚Äô, Stefan-Boltzmann Law becomes:

\[M_{out}=ŒµœÉT_K^{4}\]

Or in terms of total energy flux:

\[E_{out}=4\pi R_E^2ŒµœÉT_K^{4}\]

Recall that \(E_{in}=S_o\pi R_E^2(1-Œ±)\), and \(E_{in}=E_{out}\):

\[S_o\pi R_E^2(1-Œ±)=4\pi R_E^2ŒµœÉT_K^{4}\]

Eliminating \(\pi\) and \(R_E^2\) gives, and rearranging for \(T_K\) gives:

\[T_K=(\frac{S_o(1-Œ±)}{4ŒµœÉ})^{\frac{1}{4}}\]

Note: Œµ must be between 0 and 1, it should be 0.62 for Earth‚Äôs temperature to be 14ÀöC.

Greenhouse effect¬∂

Let‚Äôs first use binomial expansion on the equation linking absolute temperature (\(T_K\)), temperature in degrees Celsius (\(T_C\)), and conversion constant from degrees Celsius to Kelvin (\(T_o=273\)).

Recall that binomial expansion is \((1+x)^{n}‚âà(1+nx)\) when x<<1:

\[T_K=(T_o+T_C)\]
\[T_K^4=(T_o+T_C)^4=T_o^4(1+\frac{T_C}{T_o})^{4}‚âàT_o^4(1+4\frac{T_C}{T_o})\]

Substitute \(T_K\) into Stefan-Boltzmann Law:

\[M_{out}=œÉT_K^{4}‚âàœÉ(T_o^4(1+4\frac{T_C}{T_o}))\]

In terms of total energy flux:

\[E_{out}=œÉ4\pi R_E^{2}(T_o^4(1+4\frac{T_C}{T_o}))\]

Including emissivity:

\[E_{out}=ŒµœÉ4\pi R_E^{2}(T_o^4(1+4\frac{T_C}{T_o}))\]
\[=4\pi R_E^{2}(ŒµœÉT_o^{4}+4ŒµœÉT_o^{3}T_C)\]
\[=4\pi R_E^{2}(a+bT_C)\]

where \(a=ŒµœÉT_o^{4}\) and \(b=4ŒµœÉT_o^{3}\) are constants.

a is the constant coefficient for longwave radiation \(= ŒµœÉT_o^{4}\) \(Wm^{-2}\)

b is temperature-dependent coefficient for longwave radiation \(= 4ŒµœÉT_o^{3}\) \(Wm^{-2}ÀöC^{-1}\)

Since \(E_{in}=E_{out}\) at radiative equilibrium:

\[S_o\pi R_E^2(1-Œ±)=4\pi R_E^{2}(a+bT_C)\]
\[S_o(1-Œ±)=4(a+bT_c)\]
\[\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)=a+bT_c\]

In the equation above, LHS gives energy flux density into the Earth, and RHS gives energy flux density out of the Earth.

Rearrange for \(T_C\) gives:

\[T_C=\frac{\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-a}{b}\]

We can rewrite the equation above as:

\[T_C = \frac{M_{in}-a}{b} = \frac{M_{in}}{b}-\frac{a}{b}\]

Since \(\frac{a}{b}\) is constant, we can write this in terms of change in temperature and change in incoming energy flux:

\[‚àÜT_C = \frac{‚àÜM_{in}}{b}\]

Therefore, the change in temperature is proportional to radiative forcing, the difference in energy flux density going in and out of the Earth.

The radiative forcing associated with CO\(_{2}\) gas of concentration [CO\(_2\)] compared to a base level concentration ot [CO\(_2\)]\(_0\) can be approximated by:

\[‚àÜM_{CO_2,in} = 5.35ln(\frac{[CO_2]}{[CO_2]_0})\]

For a doubling of CO\(_2\), the radiative forcing would be:

\[‚àÜM_{CO_2,in} = 5.35ln(2) = 3.7 Wm^{-2}\]

Substituting this back into the equation fo \(T_C\):

\[‚àÜT_{CO2} = \frac{3.7}{b}\]

The change in temperature from doubling concentration of CO\(_2\) compared to pre-industrial levels is climate sensibility.

Simplified approximations of radiative forcing caused by other greenhouse gases can be found in Table 3 in the paper Myhre et al.

def find_newT(CO2_now, CO2_initial=280, e=0.62, s=5.67e-8, T_o=273, S_o=1365, al=0.3):
    dM = 5.35 * np.log(CO2_now/CO2_initial)
    M = 0.25*S_o*(1-al) + dM
    a = e * s * T_o**4
    b = 4 * e * s * T_o**3
    return (M - a) / b

Storing heat¬∂

Recall that heat capacity (C) is the energy (E) required to raise the temperature of a body of a material by 1ÀöC, independent of mass.

\(C=\frac{E}{‚àÜT}\) \(JÀöC^{-1}\)

Given that most of the temperature change is due to the heat exchange with the top 70m layer of the ocean, we‚Äôll assume the contribution from land and deep ocean to heat storage is negligible, and that the ocean is 70m deep.

The density of seawater \(œÅ=1025kgm^{-3}\), specific heat capacity of water \(c=4200Jkg^{-1}ÀöC^{-1}\), the fraction (\(f\)) which the Earth‚Äôs surface is covered by ocean (70%), and depth of the ocean (\(d\)), we can calculate the heat capacity of the ocean per meter squared:

\[C=fœÅdc\]

You can verify this by looking at the units.

Substituting in the numbers, the Earth‚Äôs heat capacity is \(2.11*10^{8}Jm^{-2}ÀöC^{-1}\).

Restoring equilibrium¬∂

The first law of thermodynamics states that the total energy in an isolated system is constant.

Treating the climate system as an isolated system, the rate of change in internal energy of the climate system must be balanced by the rate of net heating or cooling.

From above, we derived that energy flux density absorbed \(M_{in}=\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)\), and energy flux density out \(M_{out}=(a+bT_C)\), thus net heating/cooling is:

\[M_{in}-M_{out}=\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-(a+bT_C)\]

The change in temperature per unit area per unit time is \(C\frac{dT}{dt}\) (verify this by units).

Change in temperature is equivalent to change in kinetic energy i.e. internal energy of the system.

Therefore:

\[C\frac{dT}{dt}=\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-(a+bT_C)\]
\[\frac{dT}{dt}=\frac{\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-(a+bT_C)}{C}\]

To simplify, since T_C is merely a specific value of T:

\[\frac{dT}{dt}=\frac{\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-(a+bT)}{C}\]

This equation accounts for the fact that when the system is out of equilibrium for whatever reason, it will try and restore equilibrium.

Solving this differential equation gives:

\[T=\frac{1}{b}(\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-a-CDe^{-\frac{b}{c}t})=\frac{1}{b}(M_{in}-a-CDe^{-\frac{b}{c}t})\]

where D is an arbitrary constant determined by initial conditions:

\[D=\frac{\frac{1}{4}S_o(1-Œ±)-a-T_{t=0}b}{C}\]
# define a function to calculate temp over time
def equilibrium_T(T_initial, t, e=0.62, al=0.3, C=2.11e8, S_o=1365, s=5.67e-8, T_o=273):
    a = e * s * T_o**4
    b = 4 * e * s * T_o**3
    D = (0.25*S_o*(1-al)-a-T_initial*b)/(C)
    t = t*3600*24*365 # convert t from years to seconds
    return (1/b)*(0.25*S_o*(1-al)-a-C*D*np.exp(-b*t/C))

Ice-albedo positive feedback - tipping points¬∂

Assume that due to some perturbation:

  • the average temperature becomes lower than -10ÀöC, then the entire Earth freezes, albedo becomes 0.9

  • the average temperature is between -10ÀöC and 10ÀöC, then the albedo is between 0.3 and 0.9, and this can be calculated by using ratios:

\[Œ±=Œ±_{ice}-(Œ±_{ice}-Œ±_{land})*\frac{T_{now}-T_{freeze}}{T_{melt}-T_{freeze}}\]

  • the average temperare becomes higher than 10ÀöC, then the entire Earth‚Äôs ice melts, albedo becomes 0.3

When a tipping point is reached e.g. temperature changes from 10ÀöC to 11ÀöC, albedo changes, leading to drastic changes in energy balance, thus equilibrium temperature also changes.

def find_al(T_initial, T_freeze=-10, T_melt=10, al_ice=0.9, al_land=0.3):
    if T_initial <= T_freeze:
        al = al_ice
    elif T_initial >= T_melt:
        al = al_land
    else:
        al = al_ice - (al_ice-al_land)*(T_initial-T_freeze)/(T_melt-T_freeze)
    return al

Final model¬∂

Now, let‚Äôs include all the concepts above into this final model.

Note that CO\(_2\) is the only greenhouse gas considered, and the initial conditions are set to temperature of 14ÀöC and CO\(_2\) concentration of 280ppm. Feel free to change them and other variables in the model.

def simple_model(final_co2, years, current_temp=14, current_co2=280, e=0.62, S_o=1365, T_o=273, s=5.67e-8, f=0.7, d=70, p=1025, c=4200):
    temperature = [14]
    t = 0
    total_t = years
    al = 0.3
    dM_in = 5.35 * np.log(final_co2/current_co2)
    C = f * d * p * c
    a = e * s * T_o**4
    b = 4 * e * s * T_o**3
    while t < total_t:
        t += 1
        seconds = t * 3600 * 24 * 365.25
        if temperature[-1] <= -10:
            al = 0.9
        elif temperature[-1] >= 10:
            al = 0.3
        else:
            al = 0.9 - (0.9-0.3)*(temperature[-1]+10)/(20)
        M_in = 0.25*S_o*(1-al) + dM_in
        D = (M_in-a-current_temp*b)/(C)
        temp = ((1/b)*(M_in-a-C*D*np.exp(-b*seconds/C)))
        temperature.append(temp)
    return temperature

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
ax1 = fig.add_subplot(111)
ax1.margins(0.1)

t = np.array([i for i in range(time)])

colour = ['r', 'orange', 'y', 'g', 'b', 'purple', 'c', 'm', 'k']

# plot figure
import random

for i in range(len(all_CO2_conc)):
    ax1.plot(t, projections['CO2 concentration='+str(all_CO2_conc[i])], random.choice(colour), label='projection %.d' % (i+1))
ax1.set_xlabel('Time (years)')
ax1.set_ylabel('Temperature (Degrees Celsius)')
ax1.set_title('A plot of temperature over time', fontsize=16)

Conclusion¬∂

Of course this model is still very crude. There are still plenty of things that it doesn‚Äôt take into account, such as clouds, position of continents, vegetation, etc. In a real climate model, other factors also have to be included such as atmospheric dynamics, climate variability, human activity, and much more.

However, this example shows how scientists build climate models by using physical equations, making approximations and aassumptions, inserting parameters into those equations to account for any variability, and adjusting their values so that it resembles the real world every time it is run.
